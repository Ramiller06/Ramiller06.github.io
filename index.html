Case Study

Pristine Rides —
Lead Gen Campaign

Pristine Rides is a mobile detailing marketplace connecting Seattle-area customers with trusted local detailers. I built and ran the Google Ads lead generation campaign from the ground up — managing everything from campaign structure to landing page design and conversion tracking.

Project Overview

As the sole campaign manager, I owned the entire acquisition funnel — from initial account setup through ongoing optimization. This wasn't a managed service or an inherited account. Every decision was mine.

  • Google Ads account structure and campaign setup
  • Keyword research and match type strategy
  • Ad copy creation and testing
  • Landing page design and development (Carrd → Webflow)
  • Google Tag Manager implementation and container setup
  • Conversion tracking setup, verification, and debugging
  • Google Search Console configuration and sitemap submission
  • PageSpeed and Core Web Vitals optimization
  • Performance analysis and iterative optimization
  • DNS and domain configuration via Namecheap

Key takeaway: Effective paid search isn't just about driving clicks — it's about building a system you can trust to measure, learn from, and improve over time.

Phase One

Launch — Establishing the Baseline

Complete

What I Did

Built and launched the first Google Search campaign targeting local mobile detailing searches in Seattle. The landing page was a funnel-style page built on Carrd, designed to move visitors quickly toward a lead form. Daily budget was set at $4/day to test campaign viability before scaling.

I structured the campaign around high-intent local keywords and wrote ad copy focused on convenience and trust — the two things someone searching for a mobile detailer cares about most.

Results

The campaign ran to $110 total spend and generated early signals on click-through rate and lead volume. Full conversion tracking couldn't be configured on Carrd due to platform limitations around separate page URLs — a gap I identified and prioritized fixing for the next phase.

Phase Two

Iteration — Testing a New Approach

Complete

What I Did

Migrated the campaign from Carrd to Webflow and implemented end-to-end conversion tracking through GTM and Google Ads — establishing a reliable measurement foundation the campaign had been missing. The new landing page used a simplified layout with a hero section, lead form, and "how it works" explainer.

CPL came in at ~$38. Phase Three

Clean Test — Isolating the Landing Page

In Progress

The Principle Behind This Phase

Control the variables. Tracking was verified before a single dollar was spent. Ad copy was held constant to match Phase 2. A minimum click threshold was set before any conclusions would be drawn. Phase 3 is the test Phase 2 should have been.

The Setup

Built a new funnel-style landing page on Webflow with GTM conversion tracking verified before launch. Only one variable is changing — the landing page. Ad copy stays consistent with Phase 2 to create a clean comparison. Budget held at $12/day to maintain comparability.

Success Criteria

Minimum 50–60 clicks before drawing conclusions. Tracking verified end-to-end before spend begins. Target CPL below Phase 2's $38 baseline — ideally returning to Phase 1's estimated conversion rate of ~18%.

Approach

How I think about
paid search.

Running a campaign with a small budget taught me things a big budget can hide — every dollar has to earn its place.

🎯

Test one thing at a time

Changing multiple variables simultaneously makes it impossible to attribute results. I learned this firsthand in Phase 2 and it's now the first principle I apply to any test design.

📊

Respect sample size

Small budgets produce noisy data. I won't draw conclusions from 25 clicks — the numbers need room to stabilize before they mean anything actionable.

🔧

Fix tracking before spending

Phase 1's biggest lesson: unverified tracking means wasted spend. I now verify conversion tags in GTM preview mode before a campaign goes live — every time.

Challenges

What went wrong
and what I did about it.

The most valuable lessons from this campaign didn't come from the wins — they came from diagnosing the problems.

Challenge 01

Tracking limitations derailed Phase 1 data collection

The landing page was built on Carrd, which uses a single-page architecture. Without a separate thank you page URL, GTM couldn't fire a conversion event on form submission. I didn't catch this until Phase 1 was already underway. The fix was rebuilding the landing page on Webflow for Phase 2 — a platform that supported proper GTM implementation and thank you page tracking.

Challenge 02

Phase 2 had a flawed test design

I changed two variables simultaneously — the landing page layout and the ad copy. When Phase 2 performed worse than Phase 1, I couldn't isolate which change caused the drop. I identified this as a methodology error mid-phase and used it to redesign Phase 3: one variable at a time, tracking verified before launch, minimum click threshold before drawing conclusions.

Challenge 03

A pre-existing Quality Score problem surfaced mid-Phase 3

After adding Quality Score columns to my keyword view, I discovered several core keywords had historical scores of 1-2/10 — likely dating back to Phase 1. Low Quality Scores meant low Ad Rank, which meant limited impression share across all three phases. I identified two contributing factors: landing page copy that signaled a marketplace model rather than a direct service, and keyword history that couldn't be reversed mid-campaign. This informed the decision to start fresh keywords in Phase 4 rather than trying to rehabilitate suppressed ones.

Challenge 04

Real detailer feedback revealed a lead quality gap

After connecting detailers with leads, I followed up to ask what additional information would make leads more actionable. The feedback: timeframe. A customer interested in detailing "in a couple of months" requires a completely different follow-up than someone ready to book this week. I added a timeframe field to the form mid-Phase 3 — a small operational change that directly improved lead usefulness without affecting the core test variables.

Key Takeaways

What this campaign
taught me.

🧪

Test design matters more than budget

Small budgets amplify the cost of bad test design. Changing two variables at once in Phase 2 cost real money and produced inconclusive data. The discipline of isolating one variable per phase — even when it slows things down — is what makes results actually meaningful.

📡

Tracking is the foundation, not an afterthought

Phase 1's biggest lesson wasn't about ad copy or landing pages — it was that unverified tracking means untrustworthy data. Every phase after that started with a tracking verification step before a single dollar was spent.

🔁

The funnel doesn't end at the form

Staying connected to what happens after a lead submits — talking to detailers, following up on bookings, asking what information they actually need — produced insights no dashboard could. Real campaign management means owning the outcome, not just the click.
